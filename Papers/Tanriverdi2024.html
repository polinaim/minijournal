Rapid assessment of peripheral visual crowding

Tanriverdi et al. 2024

Introduction


In our day-to-day lives, we experience rapidly changing environments cluttered with information of all types. Just as when you have too many tabs open in Google Chrome, this information can have big implications on performance. Within vision, this performance reduction can be seen as a worsened ability to perceive objects accurately. In cluttered environments, this can impede our ability to recognise specific objects. This phenomenon is known as ‘visual crowding’, and it’s most impactful within outer regions of vision, or peripheral vision. For instance, Figure 1 gives a nice demonstration of visual crowding at play. When looking straight at the target in the centre of the image, it is much easier to notice the child on the right of the screen, who is in the open, rather than the child surrounded by road signs. This example shows how crowding can play a role in daily tasks such as driving.


Whilst visual crowding is not an issue for most individuals, it can become of concern for those experiencing certain visual or brain-related deficits. Namely dyslexia, schizophrenia, nystagmus, macular degeneration and glaucoma (1). In such individuals, crowding effects are more pronounced, and can negatively impact day-to-day life. It can lead to issues in reading speeds for dyslexic individuals (2), and issues in face recognition for individuals with glaucoma (3). These challenges highlight the need to increase our understanding of visual crowding, so we can tailor current treatments and diagnoses towards these issues. Such would be a welcomed change, as currently there is a heavy reliance on time-consuming and attentionally demanding techniques. This reliance on demanding techniques becomes an issue when assessing certain patient groups who tend to struggle under conventional psychophysics techniques (4). Conventional techniques typically involve presenting a visual stimulus and expecting a response which can become over-demanding due to prolonged focus on singular objects, or even too many repetitions/responses needed. =======

Introduction


The problem
Have you ever tried to find a friend in a packed area and failed? That’s visual crowding in action – when the surrounding clutter makes it hard for you to pick out specific details. For example, people with dyslexia might find it harder to read, and those with glaucoma might have trouble recognizing faces. However, current methods for assessment of visual crowding are too complex and impractical for regular clinical use, making accurate diagnosis challenging.


How did they try to solve it?
This study aimed to find quicker, easier and more accurate ways to measure visual crowding by introducing two new methods:

  • Eye Movement Response: Instead of pressing buttons or using a mouse, participants simply use their eye movements to indicate what they see.
  • Adaptive Psychophysics: This method adjusts the difficulty of the test based on the participant’s performance. If someone is doing well, the test becomes more challenging; if they’re struggling, it gets easier.
Key outcomes

Using eye movements or quick serial searches to test for visual crowding could be a faster and still reliable way to assess this issue. It might make things easier for routine checks, but further tests are needed to see how well these methods work with different groups of people and specific needs.

The Study


Our study aims to solve the aforementioned issues. We will investigate visual crowding to find a more intuitive and efficient method of testing for crowding effects, which is also suited for patient groups. If successful in this goal, we can provide a more effective method of assessing early indicators of visual impairment in various disorders. In turn, this can allow for necessary treatment to be given at an earlier stage, due to earlier diagnosis. One of our main ideas in reaching this goal is to move towards a task employing eye movement analysis. Whilst eye tracking gives a range of metrics surrounding visual functioning, we mainly want to implement eye tracking as a response mechanism. This would replace the need for a manual ‘button press’ typically seen in traditional approaches. Instead, participants would make a ‘saccade’ (rapid eye movement) towards a target, indicating a response. We believe this implementation of eye movements will allow for a freer and more natural experience for participants. Another way we aim to improve on traditional methods is by reducing the number of repetitions needed. As mentioned, psychophysical experiments typically require large amounts of repetition to gain necessary information. However, in our study, we will employ an adaptive psychophysics approach where the experiment dynamically changes difficulty based on an individual's performance on the task. This is a clever approach which allows for fewer repetitions to collect necessary information, thus reducing the overall length of the experiment.


Ultimately, our primary focus is to develop an innovative, efficient, and adaptive psychophysics task to measure visual crowding. To investigate the effectiveness of using eye movement responses, we aim to introduce different ‘visual search’ paradigms to either promote continuous eye movement responses or not. A ‘serial search’ paradigm will be implemented to promote eye movements, whilst a ‘parallel search’ paradigm will be implemented to minimise eye movements. These paradigms are a part of ‘visual search’ which denotes the methods used to analyse a visual environment. ‘Serial search’ is an inefficient searching mechanism requiring individual inspection of objects to find the target, usually adopted in situations where an object of interest doesn’t stand out from its background. On the other hand, ‘parallel search’ is a more efficient mechanism which compares multiple objects at once, and is adopted when an object does stand out from its background. By employing a ‘serial search’ paradigm in our study, we can be more sure that continuous eye movements will be present since all objects will likely be searched independently.

Methods (Optional)


Participants

The study involved 15 healthy adults aged between 19 and 32.


Equipment

  • • Screen and Computer: The experiments were done using a monitor and a MacBook Pro with MATLAB software to run the experiments.
  • • An eye tracker (a device that records where you're looking) to monitor where participants were looking during the experiment.

Test Paradigm

• Gabor Patches: The main visual elements used were Gabor patches, which are blurry, stripey patterns often used in vision research. Sometimes the target (the pattern participants needed to find) was shown by itself, and sometimes it was surrounded by other similar patches (called "flankers") to create a crowding effect, making it harder to identify the target.

• Manipulating Difficulty: The difficulty of the task was adjusted by changing the angle of the stripes in the Gabor patches. The more the stripes were tilted, the easier it was to identify the target. The researchers also varied the similarity between the target and the surrounding patches to see how it affected the crowding.


Task Variations

  • • 2AFC and 6AFC Paradigms: In the 2AFC (Two-Alternative Forced Choice) task, participants had to choose between two options—essentially deciding which of two spots had the target. In the 6AFC (Six-Alternative Forced Choice) task, they chose from six spots, which was considered more challenging.
  • • Continuous Serial Search: In this task, participants looked at a grid of 28 patches and had to identify as many targets as possible by moving their eyes over them. This task was more complex and tested their ability to search through a crowded space.

Results (Optional)

Comparison of Methods: The researchers compared both traditional and new methods by looking at three main factors...

  • Speed: How quickly the method could measure visual crowding.
  • Accuracy: How precise the results were.
  • Ease of Use: How easy the methods were for participants to complete.

Conclusions

Terminology

• Bayesian Hypothesis Testing is a statistical method that compares the probability of different hypotheses being true. Unlike traditional methods that just tell you if a result is significant or not (by rejecting or not rejecting a null hypothesis), Bayesian testing gives you a Bayes Factor, which tells you how much more likely one hypothesis is compared to another.
